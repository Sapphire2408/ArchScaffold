# Layered (N-Tier) Architecture

=== presentation
# Presentation Layer

## What
The top layer — handles HTTP requests, routing, and response formatting. It receives user input and delegates to the business layer.

## Why
Separating presentation from business logic means you can change your web framework or add new interfaces without rewriting business rules.

## What Goes Here
Use `controllers/` for request handlers, `middleware/` for HTTP concerns, `routes/` for route definitions.

=== presentation/controllers
# Controllers

## What
Request handlers that parse input, call business services, and return formatted responses.

## Why
Thin translation layer between HTTP and your business logic.

## What Goes Here
`UserController.ts`, `OrderController.ts`, `AuthController.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic, database queries, or complex transformations.

=== presentation/middleware
# Middleware

## What
Functions that intercept requests/responses for cross-cutting concerns.

## Why
Centralizes authentication, logging, error handling, and other concerns that span multiple routes.

## What Goes Here
`auth.ts`, `errorHandler.ts`, `rateLimiter.ts`, `requestLogger.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic.

=== presentation/routes
# Routes

## What
Route definitions that map HTTP endpoints to controllers.

## Why
Separating routes from controllers makes your URL structure visible at a glance and allows reorganizing endpoints without modifying handler logic.

## What Goes Here
`userRoutes.ts`, `orderRoutes.ts`, `authRoutes.ts`, `index.ts` (route aggregator).

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Request handling logic — that belongs in controllers.

=== business
# Business Layer

## What
The middle layer — contains all business logic, validation rules, and service orchestration. It depends on the data layer and is called by the presentation layer.

## Why
Isolating business logic in its own layer means it can be reused across different presentation mechanisms and tested independently.

## What Goes Here
Use `services/` for business operations, `validators/` for validation rules.

=== business/services
# Services

## What
Business services that implement your application's operations — the core logic of what your system does.

## Why
Services encapsulate business workflows. A `UserService` handles user creation, validation, and coordination between repositories.

## What Goes Here
`UserService.ts`, `OrderService.ts`, `PaymentService.ts`, `NotificationService.ts`, `AuthService.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
HTTP concerns, direct database queries (use repositories), or response formatting.

=== business/validators
# Validators

## What
Validation logic for business rules — input validation, state validation, cross-field validation.

## Why
Extracting validation keeps services focused on orchestration and makes validation rules reusable and testable.

## What Goes Here
`userValidator.ts`, `orderValidator.ts`, `paymentValidator.ts`. Schema definitions if using a validation library.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business orchestration or data access.

=== data
# Data Layer

## What
The bottom layer — handles all data persistence, database interactions, and entity definitions. It's the only layer that talks to the database.

## Why
Isolating data access means you can change your database, query strategy, or ORM without affecting business logic.

## What Goes Here
Use `repositories/` for data access objects, `entities/` for database models, `migrations/` for schema changes.

=== data/repositories
# Repositories

## What
Data access objects that encapsulate all database queries and operations for a specific entity.

## Why
Repositories provide a clean interface for data operations. The business layer calls `userRepository.findById(id)` without knowing the underlying query.

## What Goes Here
`UserRepository.ts`, `OrderRepository.ts`, `ProductRepository.ts`. Each encapsulates CRUD and custom queries for one entity.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic or HTTP concerns.

=== data/entities
# Entities

## What
Database models/schemas — the shape of your data as it exists in the database.

## Why
Separating database models from business logic prevents ORM concerns from leaking into your services.

## What Goes Here
`UserEntity.ts`, `OrderEntity.ts`, `ProductEntity.ts`. ORM model definitions, database schemas.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business rules or application logic.

=== data/migrations
# Migrations

## What
Database schema migration files — versioned changes to your database structure.

## Why
Migrations provide a controlled, reversible way to evolve your database schema over time.

## What Goes Here
Timestamped migration files like `20240101_create_users_table.ts`, `20240102_add_email_index.ts`. Seeders for initial data.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Application logic or repository code.

=== common
# Common

## What
Shared utilities, types, and helpers used across all layers.

## Why
Prevents duplication of generic functionality that doesn't belong to any specific layer.

## What Goes Here
Use `utils/` for helper functions, `types/` for shared type definitions.

=== common/utils
# Utils

## What
Generic utility functions used across the application.

## Why
Centralizes helper logic so it's not duplicated across layers.

## What Goes Here
`dateUtils.ts`, `stringUtils.ts`, `hashUtils.ts`, `paginationHelper.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business-specific logic.

=== common/types
# Types

## What
Shared type definitions, interfaces, and enums used across multiple layers.

## Why
Single source of truth for cross-layer types.

## What Goes Here
`pagination.ts`, `apiResponse.ts`, `enums.ts`, `common.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Layer-specific types.

=== config
# Config

## What
Application configuration and environment settings.

## Why
Centralizes all configuration to keep environment details out of your logic layers.

## What Goes Here
`database.config.ts`, `app.config.ts`, `environment.ts`, `logger.config.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic or route definitions.
