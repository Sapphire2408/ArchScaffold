# Event-Driven Architecture

=== events
# Events

## What
The central nervous system of the architecture. Everything revolves around events — things that happened. Components communicate by emitting and reacting to events rather than calling each other directly.

## Why
Event-driven architecture maximizes decoupling. Producers don't know about consumers. Adding a new reaction to an event means adding a new subscriber — zero changes to existing code.

## What Goes Here
Use `definitions/` for event schemas, `handlers/` for event processing logic, `publishers/` for event emission, `subscribers/` for event consumption setup.

=== events/definitions
# Event Definitions

## What
The schemas and types for all events in the system. Each event defines its name, payload structure, and metadata.

## Why
Event definitions are the contracts of your system. Producers and consumers both depend on these definitions, ensuring they agree on what data an event carries.

## What Goes Here
`UserRegistered.ts`, `OrderPlaced.ts`, `PaymentProcessed.ts`, `InventoryUpdated.ts`. Each defines the event type, payload interface, and version.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Event handling logic or infrastructure code.

=== events/handlers
# Event Handlers

## What
Functions or classes that react to specific events. Each handler performs one action in response to an event.

## Why
Handlers are where side effects live. When `OrderPlaced` fires, separate handlers might: send a confirmation email, update inventory, notify the warehouse — each independently.

## What Goes Here
`SendWelcomeEmailOnUserRegistered.ts`, `UpdateInventoryOnOrderPlaced.ts`, `NotifyWarehouseOnOrderPlaced.ts`. Naming convention: action + "On" + event.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Event definitions, publishing logic, or domain models.

=== events/publishers
# Event Publishers

## What
Components responsible for emitting events. They take a domain action's result and publish the corresponding event to the event bus or message broker.

## Why
Separating publishing logic allows you to control how events are dispatched — synchronously, asynchronously, with retry logic, or with guaranteed delivery.

## What Goes Here
`EventPublisher.ts`, `DomainEventEmitter.ts`, domain-specific publishers if needed.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Event handling or infrastructure configuration.

=== events/subscribers
# Event Subscribers

## What
Subscription setup — wiring events to their handlers. This is where you declare which handlers listen to which events.

## Why
Centralizing subscription setup makes it easy to see the full event flow at a glance — a map of "when X happens, do Y, Z, and W."

## What Goes Here
`subscriptions.ts`, or per-domain files like `orderSubscriptions.ts`, `userSubscriptions.ts`. Registration code that connects events to handlers.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Handler logic or event definitions.

=== sagas
# Sagas (Process Managers)

## What
Long-running workflows that react to sequences of events, coordinating multi-step business processes. A saga listens for events, maintains state about the workflow's progress, and triggers further actions (commands or events) to advance or compensate the process.

## Why
In event-driven systems, complex operations span multiple events and services. An order fulfillment saga might wait for `PaymentProcessed`, then emit a `ShipOrder` command, then wait for `ItemShipped`. If `PaymentFailed` arrives instead, it triggers compensating actions like `ReleaseInventory`. Without sagas, this coordination logic gets scattered across unrelated event handlers.

## What Goes Here
`OrderFulfillmentSaga.ts`, `UserOnboardingSaga.ts`, `PaymentRetrySaga.ts`, `RefundSaga.ts`. Each saga defines: (1) which event starts it, (2) what state it tracks, (3) which events advance it, (4) what actions it takes at each step, (5) how it compensates on failure.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Simple, single-step event reactions (those are event handlers), domain logic, or infrastructure code.

## Choreography vs. Orchestration
There are two styles. In **choreography**, each service reacts to events independently with no central coordinator — the workflow emerges from the chain of events. In **orchestration**, a saga acts as a central coordinator that explicitly manages the steps. This directory supports both: choreography-style sagas are lightweight (mostly just compensation logic), while orchestration-style sagas are more stateful and directive.

=== domain
# Domain

## What
Business models and services. In event-driven architecture, domain operations typically produce events as a primary output.

## Why
The domain encapsulates business logic. It processes commands and produces events that represent what happened.

## What Goes Here
Use `models/` for entities and value objects, `services/` for business operations.

=== domain/models
# Domain Models

## What
Entities and value objects. In event-driven systems, models often track uncommitted events that are flushed after persistence.

## Why
Models carry business rules. They decide whether an action is valid and what event it produces.

## What Goes Here
`User.ts`, `Order.ts`, `Product.ts`, `Money.ts`. Models may include methods like `placeOrder()` that return or queue domain events.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Infrastructure code or event handling.

=== domain/services
# Domain Services

## What
Business operations that coordinate multiple models or don't belong to a single entity.

## Why
Some operations span entities — like transferring funds between accounts. Domain services handle these while still producing events.

## What Goes Here
`OrderService.ts`, `PaymentService.ts`, `InventoryService.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Infrastructure concerns or HTTP handling.

=== infrastructure
# Infrastructure

## What
Technical implementations — message brokers, databases, and external integrations.

## Why
Infrastructure supports the event-driven flow: message brokers deliver events, databases persist state.

## What Goes Here
Use `messaging/` for event bus and broker implementations, `persistence/` for database code.

=== infrastructure/messaging
# Messaging

## What
Event bus and message broker implementations — the backbone of event delivery.

## Why
The messaging infrastructure determines how events flow: in-memory for simple cases, RabbitMQ or Kafka for distributed systems, with guarantees like at-least-once delivery.

## What Goes Here
`RabbitMQBroker.ts`, `KafkaProducer.ts`, `KafkaConsumer.ts`, `InMemoryEventBus.ts`, `EventSerializer.ts`, `DeadLetterHandler.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic or event definitions.

=== infrastructure/persistence
# Persistence

## What
Database implementations — repositories, ORM config, migrations. May include an event store if using event sourcing.

## Why
Persistence is where state lives between events. In event-sourced systems, the event store IS the primary persistence.

## What Goes Here
`PostgresUserRepository.ts`, `EventStore.ts`, ORM models, migrations, connection config.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic or messaging code.

=== api
# API

## What
HTTP layer — the entry point for external requests. API controllers dispatch domain operations that trigger the event flow.

## Why
The API translates external input into domain operations.

## What Goes Here
Use `controllers/` for request handlers, `middleware/` for HTTP concerns.

=== api/controllers
# Controllers

## What
HTTP request handlers that trigger domain operations. The response might be synchronous or the controller might return immediately and let events handle the rest.

## Why
Controllers bridge HTTP to your event-driven internals.

## What Goes Here
`UserController.ts`, `OrderController.ts`, `WebhookController.ts` (for incoming external events).

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic or event handling.

=== api/middleware
# Middleware

## What
Cross-cutting HTTP concerns.

## Why
Centralizes authentication, logging, error handling.

## What Goes Here
`auth.ts`, `errorHandler.ts`, `rateLimiter.ts`, `requestLogger.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic.

=== config
# Config

## What
Application configuration, broker connection settings, and dependency injection.

## Why
Centralizes all configuration including messaging infrastructure setup.

## What Goes Here
`container.ts`, `broker.config.ts`, `database.config.ts`, `environment.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Business logic.

=== shared
# Shared

## What
Cross-cutting utilities and types.

## Why
Generic helpers used across the event-driven system.

## What Goes Here
`Result.ts`, `DateUtils.ts`, `constants.ts`, `EventMetadata.ts`.

## What Does NOT Belong Here
Domain-specific code.
